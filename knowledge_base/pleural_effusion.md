# Pleural Effusion on Chest Radiograph (digest)

Source: BTS Pleural Disease guidance, adapted for primary-care triage
Source-ID: PLEURAL-TRIAGE
Topic: pleural effusion, blunted costophrenic angle, meniscus sign, pleural fluid

## Radiographic pattern
Blunting of the costophrenic angle is the earliest sign; a concave upper border
(meniscus sign) appears with larger effusions. Around 200-300 mL is needed before
blunting is visible on an erect PA film. Massive effusion causes opacification of
a hemithorax with possible mediastinal shift away from the effusion.

## Common causes by context
- Transudates: heart failure, hypoalbuminemia, renal failure.
- Exudates: parapneumonic effusion/empyema, tuberculosis, malignancy. In a
  TB-endemic setting a unilateral exudative effusion in a young adult is TB until
  proven otherwise.

## Triage actions at a primary health centre
- Assess respiratory status and SpO2. Note unilateral versus bilateral; bilateral
  effusions point toward a systemic transudative cause.
- Refer for diagnostic pleural aspiration and fluid analysis where available.

## When to escalate urgently
Large effusion with respiratory distress, hypoxia, suspected empyema (fever plus
effusion), or mediastinal shift warrants urgent referral.
