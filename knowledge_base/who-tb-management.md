# Tuberculosis: Detection and Management at Primary Care Level

source: WHO Consolidated Guidelines on Tuberculosis (Module 3: Diagnosis; Module 4: Treatment)
Source-ID: WHO-TB-NTP
version: 2022 update
region_note: Bangladesh National TB Control Programme (NTP) aligned

## When to suspect pulmonary TB

Suspect pulmonary tuberculosis in any patient presenting with:
- Cough persisting more than 2 weeks
- Unexplained fever, drenching night sweats, or weight loss
- Hemoptysis (coughing blood) of any amount
- Chest X-ray showing upper-lobe infiltrates, cavitation, or miliary pattern
- Close contact with a known TB case within the past 2 years

In high-burden settings such as Bangladesh, a chest X-ray suggestive of TB
warrants bacteriological confirmation even when cough duration is shorter.

## Diagnostic pathway

1. Collect sputum for a WHO-recommended rapid molecular test (GeneXpert
   MTB/RIF or Truenat) as the FIRST test — not smear microscopy alone.
2. A radiograph compatible with TB plus a negative rapid test does not
   exclude TB; refer for clinical evaluation and possible culture.
3. Test for rifampicin resistance at the same time as initial diagnosis.
4. Offer HIV testing to every patient evaluated for TB.

## Treatment principles (drug-susceptible TB)

- Standard regimen: 2 months HRZE (isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide,
  ethambutol) followed by 4 months HR, dosed by weight band.
- Treatment is provided free of charge through the NTP network; enrol the
  patient at the nearest DOTS centre on the day of diagnosis.
- Never start a single added drug to a failing regimen.
- Counsel on adherence: interruption beyond 2 weeks requires re-evaluation.

## Infection control at the facility

- Triage coughing patients to a ventilated waiting area, ideally outdoors.
- Collect sputum outside, away from other patients.
- Health workers should wear respirators when supervising sputum collection.
